## Short summary
*Briefly describe the purpose of the proposal and the software project(s) it involves. 3,000 characters maximum* 

Human movement data embeds meaningful signals about neural function and physical health, and quantitative motion analysis can measure these signals to extract insights relevant to research in neuroscience, rehabilitation, biomechanics, and motor control. However, high-resolution 3D motion analysis remains difficult to access. Marker-based systems are widely used as laboratory references, but their cost, specialized hardware, and infrastructure requirements limit adoption outside well-resourced labs. Markerless motion capture, using AI-based pose estimation, holds high potential for accessible measurement of human movement beyond specialized environments. This proposal supports FreeMoCap, a free, open-source markerless motion-capture platform that captures research-grade 3D movement data from standard cameras. Users can build systems from as few as two consumer webcams while keeping secure, local access to their data.

This proposal aims to develop FreeMoCap with community-extensible infrastructure for AI-enabled movement analysis, allowing researchers to integrate, validate, and share pose-estimation models for large-scale 3D movement research. Pose estimation (i.e., the use of trained neural networks to identify anatomical landmark locations in images) is the core AI technology enabling modern markerless motion capture. In a typical markerless pipeline, a pose-estimation model (such as Google's open-source MediaPipe) detects 2D joint positions in each camera view, which are then triangulated into 3D movement data. However, pose-estimation models are not interchangeable. Reconstruction accuracy is heavily influenced by which model is used, and different models may be better suited for specific movements, populations, or research questions depending on the data they were trained on

To enable widespread scientific use of markerless capture, open infrastructure is needed for integrating, validating, and sharing pose-estimation models. Many systems use fixed backends; if a researcher needs a different model, they must rebuild substantial pipeline components — video processing, calibration, 3D reconstruction, and analysis. FreeMoCap addresses this as a shared framework connecting AI pose-estimation to calibrated 3D reconstruction and biomechanical analysis.

FreeMoCap already supports multiple backends including MediaPipe, RTMPose, and ViTPose, and validation has revealed critical behavioral differences across research domains. Funding will support stewardship and development to make this architecture robust for broad use: formalizing backend interfaces and contribution protocols; defining standardized data structures for recordings, calibrations, keypoints, 3D trajectories, skeleton models, metadata, and quality metrics; improving programmatic and batch-processing workflows; and releasing benchmarking tools for comparing backends against specific research needs. The goal is to transition FreeMoCap from a system where core developers add backends to a community framework where researchers can rigorously contribute, validate, and share pose-estimation models for all users.

## Expected value
*If the proposal is successfully funded, what does success look like? Success means a life sciences researcher — in gait analysis, rehabilitation, neuroscience, or motor control — can capture movement data with consumer hardware and make an empirically informed choice about which pose estimation backend suits their research question, using tools that are free, open, and reproducible.

Sustained development would produce: (1) A production-grade plugin system for pose estimation backends, so new models can be made available to movement scientists without downstream pipeline changes. (2) A reusable benchmarking toolkit any group can run against their own ground-truth data, producing standardized accuracy reports across backends and tasks. (3) Structured data outputs with quality metadata and backend provenance, making FreeMoCap data directly usable in ML pipelines for training, fine-tuning, or evaluation. 

This work bridges a significant gap between the computer vision and movement science communities. New pose-estimation models are continuously developed by CV researchers but rarely reach the scientists who would use them — there is no shared infrastructure for translating AI advances into movement research tools. An extensible, contribution-friendly framework gives CV researchers a direct path to disseminate models to life sciences users, while giving movement scientists benchmarking tools to evaluate them. This sustains the only fully open-source, end-to-end motion capture system validated across multiple life sciences domains, ensuring it remains available and evolving for its community.

Researchers who need quantitative 3D movement data typically rely on proprietary marker-based systems such as Vicon, Qualisys, Motion Analysis, and OptiTrack, or commercial markerless systems such as Theia3D. Theia3D is the most validated commercial system but requires proprietary cameras (Qualisys Miqus/Vicon Vue), a closed-source pose estimation algorithm, and licenses ~$15K–$30K. OpenCap is open-source but requires iOS devices (~$1,000+ for multi-camera),  is locked to a single backend, and relies on cloud processing.

FreeMoCap occupies a distinct role as a free, open-source, hardware-agnostic platform that integrates synchronized video acquisition, camera calibration, AI-based pose estimation, multiview 3D reconstruction, skeletal representation, visualization, and biomechanical analysis. It is designed not only as an end-user motion-capture tool, but as a framework for integrating, comparing, and validating multiple AI pose-estimation backends.

FreeMoCap has demonstrated substantial adoption by the community, with over 12,000 users across aproximately 140 countries (based on optional telemetry data since September 2022), ~53% returning users, over 8,000 Github stars and near 800 Github forks, and an active forum community of over 3,000 members on Discord. Independent publications span reaching kinematics, gait mechanics, jump validation, clinical gait assessment, and computational linguistics.
