## Short summary
*Briefly describe the purpose of the proposal and the software project(s) it involves. 3,000 characters maximum* 

The FreeMoCap Project, or FreeMoCap, is a free, open-source markerless motion capture software using AI-based image tracking and synchronized camera footage to generate research-grade 3D movement data. By replacing specialized hardware and proprietary software with standard cameras and open software, FreeMoCap makes quantitative motion analysis accessible to labs, clinics, classrooms and field sites without access to traditional motion capture systems costing tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars. FreeMoCap is the core software project of the FreeMoCap Foundation, a nonprofit organization supporting open, accessible tools for for scientific, educational, and technological use. 

This proposal aims to expand FreeMoCap from an accessible human-centered motion-capture application into a more general-purpose framework for AI-enabled 3D motion capture across the life sciences. Advances in computer vision and AI have made it possible to track movement from video, but most existing markerless motion-capture tools remain limited to fixed human models, fixed body landmarks, or narrow analysis workflows. This limits their usefulness for researchers studying nonstandard human populations, assistive devices, animal behavior, or task-specific movements that require custom landmarks or tracking models.

Early extensions of FreeMoCap show what this broader framework can unlock. We have integrated and validated multiple human pose-estimation models within the same 3D reconstruction workflow, revealing that model choice can meaningfully affect downstream movement measurements. We have also extended the system to custom-trained DeepLabCut models, including work tracking freely moving ferrets in cage environments. This proposal will convert these proof-of-capability extensions into robust, documented, reusable pathways for researchers who need custom tracking models, skeletons, keypoints, or validation workflows.

Funding will move FreeMoCap’s custom motion-capture capabilities from demonstrated research extensions into stable, community-usable software. We will build clear pathways for researchers to add custom AI tracking models, define new skeletons and keypoint sets, process recordings at scale, and export standardized 3D movement data with quality metrics. We will also develop benchmarking and validation workflows so users can evaluate whether a model or pipeline is appropriate for their organism, task, or scientific question. Together, these tools will support large-scale movement datasets, reproducible analyses, and downstream AI workflows while making FreeMoCap a general-purpose open-source platform for AI-enabled 3D motion capture across the life sciences.

Success means a life-sciences researcher can use FreeMoCap to capture 3D movement data for organisms and contexts that current markerless tools do not support. A neuroscientist studying freely moving animals, a clinician assessing gait with a prosthetic limb, or an ecologist recording locomotion in a field setting could use an AI tracking model suited to their subject within FreeMoCap’s calibrated reconstruction pipeline, validate its accuracy, and produce standardized 3D data. Today, this often requires building custom infrastructure from scratch; this proposal makes it an open, supported workflow.

Upstream, the project gives AI model developers a direct path into calibrated 3D research workflows. Downstream, it produces structured outputs with quality metadata for biomechanics analyses, behavioral pipelines, statistical workflows, and machine-learning systems. Benchmarking tools will help researchers evaluate whether a model and camera configuration are appropriate for their organism, task, or scientific question before committing to a full study.

The work supports AI enablement and large-scale data analysis by making FreeMoCap programmable and batch-capable, so researchers can process large recording sets, build movement datasets across subjects and conditions, and integrate FreeMoCap into scripted or automated workflows. The result is that advances in AI-based tracking, wherever they originate, can reach movement researchers across the life sciences through shared, validated, open infrastructure.

These tools are mature and widely used, but they are costly, closed-source, and difficult for researchers to modify, audit, or extend. FreeMoCap has demonstrated substantial adoption by the community, with over 12,000 users across approximately 140 countries (based on optional telemetry data since September 2022), over 8,000 Github stars and near 800 Github forks, and an active forum community of over 3,000 members on Discord. Independent publications span reaching kinematics, gait mechanics, jump validation, clinical gait assessment, and computational linguistics. 

This proposal builds on FreeMoCap’s existing adoption by strengthening the parts of the software that make open-source scientific use possible: clear interfaces, reproducible benchmarks, quality metrics, and contribution pathways. As AI pose-estimation models evolve, these tools will help researchers evaluate which models are appropriate for their specific movement tasks and make validated improvements available to the broader community.
