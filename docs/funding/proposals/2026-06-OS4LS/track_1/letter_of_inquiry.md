## Short summary
*Briefly describe the purpose of the proposal and the software project(s) it involves. 3,000 characters maximum* 

Human movement data embeds meaningful signals about neural function, physical health, and motor behavior. Quantitative motion analysis can measure these signals to support research in neuroscience, rehabilitation, biomechanics, and related life-sciences fields, but high-resolution 3D motion analysis remains difficult to access. Marker-based systems are widely used as laboratory references, but their cost, specialized hardware, trained operators, and infrastructure requirements limit adoption outside well-resourced labs. Markerless motion capture, using AI-based pose estimation, offers a path toward more accessible measurement of human movement beyond specialized environments. This proposal supports FreeMoCap, a free, open-source markerless motion-capture platform and the core software project of the FreeMoCap Foundation. FreeMoCap captures research-grade 3D movement data from standard cameras, allowing users to build systems from as few as two consumer webcams while keeping secure, local access to their data.

This proposal aims to develop FreeMoCap as a community-extensible platform for AI-enabled movement analysis, allowing researchers to integrate, validate, and share pose-estimation models through a rigorous open-source contribution pathway. Pose estimation (i.e., the use of trained neural networks to identify anatomical landmark locations in images) is the core AI technology enabling modern markerless motion capture. In a typical markerless pipeline, a pose-estimation model (such as Google's open-source MediaPipe) detects 2D joint positions in each camera view, which are then triangulated into 3D movement data. However, pose-estimation models are not interchangeable. Reconstruction accuracy is heavily influenced by which model is used, and different models may be better suited for specific movements, populations, or research questions depending on the data they were trained on

To enable widespread scientific use of markerless capture, open infrastructure is needed for integrating, validating, and sharing pose-estimation models. Many markerless systems are built around fixed backends. If a researcher needs a different model, they must often rebuild substantial parts of the pipeline themselves. As a result, promising AI models can remain disconnected from the movement scientists who need them.

FreeMoCap already supports multiple backends including MediaPipe, RTMPose, and ViTPose, and validation has revealed critical behavioral differences across research domains. Funding will support stewardship and development to make this architecture robust for broad use: formalizing backend interfaces and contribution protocols; defining standardized data structures for recordings, calibrations, keypoints, 3D trajectories, skeleton models, metadata, and quality metrics; improving programmatic and batch-processing workflows; and releasing benchmarking tools for comparing backends against specific research needs. The goal is to transition FreeMoCap from a system where core developers add backends to a community framework where researchers can rigorously contribute, validate, and share pose-estimation models for all users.

Success means that a life-sciences researcher studying a new movement task, clinical population, or experimental setup can choose an appropriate pose-estimation backend, run it through FreeMoCap’s calibrated 3D reconstruction workflow, inspect standardized quality and accuracy reports, and export data for downstream analysis. Instead of being constrained to a single built-in model, researchers will have a rigorous open-source pathway for applying, comparing, and sharing AI pose-estimation models in 3D movement research.

Sustained development would produce: (1) A production-grade plugin system for pose estimation backends, so new models can be made available to movement scientists without downstream pipeline changes. (2) A reusable benchmarking toolkit any group can run against their own ground-truth data, producing standardized accuracy reports across backends and tasks. (3) Structured data outputs with quality metadata and backend provenance, making FreeMoCap data directly usable in ML pipelines for training, fine-tuning, or evaluation. 

This work bridges computer vision and movement science. New pose-estimation models are continuously developed, but life-sciences researchers often lack a practical route for evaluating and using them in calibrated 3D workflows. A contribution-friendly FreeMoCap framework gives model developers a path to disseminate tools to movement researchers, while giving those researchers the validation infrastructure needed to decide whether a model is appropriate for their scientific use case. This will help keep FreeMoCap open, extensible, and responsive to the communities that depend on it.

These tools are mature and widely used, but they are costly, closed-source, and difficult for researchers to modify, audit, or extend. FreeMoCap has demonstrated substantial adoption by the community, with over 12,000 users across approximately 140 countries (based on optional telemetry data since September 2022), over 8,000 Github stars and near 800 Github forks, and an active forum community of over 3,000 members on Discord. Independent publications span reaching kinematics, gait mechanics, jump validation, clinical gait assessment, and computational linguistics. 

This proposal builds on FreeMoCap’s existing adoption by strengthening the parts of the software that make open-source scientific use possible: clear interfaces, reproducible benchmarks, quality metrics, and contribution pathways. As AI pose-estimation models evolve, these tools will help researchers evaluate which models are appropriate for their specific movement tasks and make validated improvements available to the broader community.
